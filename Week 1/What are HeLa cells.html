What are HeLa cells and why are they so important?

  • HeLa cells are the most widely used huamn cell line in biological research and have been critical for many biomedical breakthrough's
  • When Henrietta's cells were taken without her consent they were given to a Physician George Otto Grey. He observed that her cancers cells were different to normal ones in that they reproduced at a very high rate (rather than dividing a few times then dying off).
  • They became known as HeLa cells, because Gey's standard method for labeling samples was to use the first two letters of the patient's first and last names
  • HeLa cells were the first human cells to be successfully cloned in 1953 by Theodore Puck and Philip I. Marcus at the University of Colorado, Denver.Since that time, HeLa cells have "continually been used for research into cancer, AIDS, the effects of radiation and toxic substances, gene mapping, and countless other scientific pursuits.
  • 1954, Jonas Salk was using HeLa cells in his research to develop the polio vaccine. To test his new vaccine, the cells were mass-produced in the first-ever cell production factory
  • Chester M. Southam, a leading virologist, injected HeLa cells into cancer patients, prison inmates, and healthy individuals in order to observe whether cancer could be transmitted as well as to examine if one could become immune to cancer by developing an acquired immune response.
  • In 1953, a lab mistake involving mixing HeLa cells with the wrong liquid allowed researchers to see and count each chromosome clearly in the HeLa cells they were working with for the first time. The accidental discovery led scientists Joe Hin Tjio and Albert Levan to develop better techniques for staining and counting chromosomes.They were the first to accurately describe that humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes rather than 24, as was previously believed. This was important for the study of developmental disorders such as Downs syndrome that involved the number of chromosomes.
